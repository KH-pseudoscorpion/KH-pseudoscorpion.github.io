Research

01

Biogeography

East Asia is a geologically complex region characterised by intricate geomorphological structures and a dynamic geographical history. These deep-time geological processes have profoundly shaped the dispersal and vicariance of animal lineages throughout the region.

Pseudoscorpions are ancient arachnids with origins dating to the Devonian. Their characteristically low dispersal ability and high degree of endemism make them particularly well-suited model organisms for studying historical biogeography. In my Master's research, I applied Sanger sequencing to reconstruct the biogeographical and evolutionary history of Allochthonius Chamberlin, 1929 — the most species-rich pseudoscorpion genus of the Korean Peninsula. In my ongoing Ph.D. research, I am extending this approach through population genomics of Anchigarypus japonicus (Beier, 1953), with the aim of elucidating the demographic and biogeographic history of this coastal species across East Asia.

02

Taxonomy

Taxonomy provides the systematic framework for identifying and understanding biodiversity. Pseudoscorpions are well known for their narrow range endemism, yet the diversity of the order within the Korean Peninsula remains substantially underestimated.

Neighbouring countries host three to eight times more described species than Korea — approximately 100 species in Japan and over 200 in China, compared to only 29 currently recorded from the Korean Peninsula. I am actively working to document undescribed species across diverse habitats, from subterranean caves to coastal seashores, using an integrative taxonomic approach that combines morphological examination with molecular data, including DNA barcoding and species delimitation analyses (ASAP, GMYC, BPP), to accurately assess species boundaries and reveal cryptic diversity.

03

Genomics

Despite being one of the oldest arachnid orders, pseudoscorpions remain among the most poorly studied groups in terms of genomic research. Genomic resources for the order are almost entirely absent, leaving fundamental questions about their evolution unresolved.

My genomic research spans multiple scales of biological organisation — from macroevolutionary patterns across the order to microevolutionary processes within populations. I am interested in genome assembly, phylogenomics, and population genomics as tools to address questions about diversification, historical demography, and lineage boundaries in pseudoscorpions.
